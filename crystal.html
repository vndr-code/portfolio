CrystalCell Simulation

Flexible Framework for Metal Crystal Formation in 3D Printing

CrystalCell Simulation is a computational framework designed to simulate the formation and evolution of metal crystals during 3D printing. The system was originally developed to study metal crystal growth, but its flexible design allows it to be adapted for a variety of crystal systems (e.g. perovskites, semiconductors, and ceramic composites).

The project addresses key research questions such as:

  • How do process parameters—such as heat, chemical environment, and mechanical stress—affect crystal nucleation and growth?
  • How can gaps and overlaps in the crystallized structure be detected and quantified?
  • What methods can be used to iteratively fill voids in the crystalline network, ensuring high structural integrity?

Approach: The simulation combines techniques from computational geometry and particle-based dynamics: - A cylinder is defined as the domain, and particles (representing nucleation sites) are packed into it using efficient spatial queries (KD-trees). - The particles are tagged based on their position relative to the cylinder’s surface and along its axial direction, mimicking how actual metal crystals form under controlled conditions. - Convex hulls are computed to define individual crystal cells, while advanced sampling and clustering techniques analyze gaps and overlap between cells.

Visualizations include animated GIFs showing crystal growth and sequential appearance, a density heat map that reveals local crystal concentration, and overlap analysis that informs the simulation’s refinement process.

This framework not only provides insight into metal crystal formation during 3D printing but also serves as a versatile tool for exploring the influence of different processing conditions on microstructural evolution in diverse material systems.

Technologies & Techniques: The project leverages particle-based dynamics, KD-tree spatial querying, convex hull construction, and clustering (via K-means) to build and analyze the crystalline structure. While it incorporates aspects of unsupervised learning (K-means clustering), its primary focus is on simulation and computational geometry.
