Mathematical Mesh Design

This section explores how mathematical transformations and complex functions generate intricate 3D structures. By extending the principles used in mathematical color patterns to three-dimensional space, these meshes emerge as computationally generated landscapes, freeform architectural shells, or abstract geometries.

Mathematical Approach

  • Complex Function Mappings: Transforming points in a 3D coordinate system using iterative function applications.
  • Symmetry Operations: Mirroring and repeating structures to create self-similar patterns.
  • Phase Portrait Techniques: Utilizing mathematical phase analysis to guide deformations.
  • Möbius Transformations & Warping: Creating controlled distortions that preserve geometric continuity while introducing curvature.

From Equations to 3D Forms

The computed points are mapped into a discrete 3D mesh, where each vertex follows a structured transformation. The generated meshes resemble:

  • Organic Terrains: Patterns that replicate natural undulating landscapes.
  • Freeform Shells: Structures mimicking architectural shells seen in parametric design.
  • Architectural Surfaces: Novel façade elements or lightweight structural forms.

Digital Fabrication Potential

These mathematical designs have real-world applications in architecture, 3D printing, and digital fabrication:

  • Meshes can be optimized for fabrication, ensuring smooth transitions and minimal distortions.
  • Certain patterns can be adapted into panelized systems, useful in façade design or modular structures.
  • The approach can inform structural simulations, examining mathematically derived shapes under real-world forces.

Demonstration

The included visualizations showcase different iterations of mathematically generated meshes, illustrating the diversity of outputs that emerge from subtle changes in mathematical formulations. The forms are generated algorithmically, ensuring high precision while allowing customization through parameter adjustments.
